# Saudi Arabia Business Law — Reference Guide

## Legal System
The Kingdom of Saudi Arabia (KSA) operates under an Islamic law (Sharia) framework,
supplemented by Royal Decrees and government regulations. Sharia is the constitution.
The legal system is a hybrid: Sharia courts handle civil and criminal matters;
specialist courts (Commercial Court, Labour Court, Administrative Court) handle specific
commercial, employment, and administrative disputes.

Vision 2030 (launched 2016) has driven major regulatory reforms, including:
- 100% foreign ownership permitted in most sectors (via MISA licensing)
- Regional Headquarters (RHQ) programme (launched 2021)
- Privatisation of state-owned enterprises
- NEOM, Red Sea, Diriyah, Qiddiya giga-projects
- Capital markets and financial sector liberalisation

Primary courts:
- Commercial Courts (in major cities): formed 2019, handle commercial disputes
- Labour Tribunals: handle employment disputes
- Grievances Board / Administrative Courts
- Supreme Court (highest appellate court for Sharia matters)
- SCCA (Saudi Center for Commercial Arbitration): ADR mechanism

## Key Statutes for Business

### Companies Law (Royal Decree No. M/3, 1437H / 2016, and updates)
- Governs all types of companies in KSA
- LLC (Sharikat Dhat Mas'ouliyyah Mahdoodah): most common for foreign investors
- Minimum 2 shareholders; maximum 50 shareholders
- Minimum capital: no statutory minimum for most activities (sector-specific requirements apply)
- Board of Directors: required; Saudi national participation encouraged but not always mandatory
- Annual financial statements: required and filed with Ministry of Commerce

### Investment Law (Royal Decree No. M/1, 1442H / 2021)
- Replaced Foreign Capital Investment Law — liberalises foreign investment
- MISA (Ministry of Investment of Saudi Arabia) issues investment licences
- Foreign investors generally permitted 100% ownership across most sectors
- Negative list: activities restricted or prohibited for foreign investors (e.g., oil exploration has special rules)
- Investment licences: MISA issues within specific timeframe

### Labour Law (Royal Decree No. M/51, 1426H / 2005, as amended)
- Applies to all private sector employees (Saudi nationals and expatriates)
- Employment contracts: must be in writing; must be in Arabic (or bilingual)
- Probationary period: maximum 90 days (extendable to 180 days by mutual agreement)
- Termination: employer can terminate with cause (Article 80 violations); without cause requires notice + severance
- Notice period: 60 days for indefinite-term contracts
- End of service gratuity: 1/2 month's wage per year (first 5 years); 1 month per year thereafter
- Annual leave: 21 days/year (<5 years service); 30 days/year (≥5 years)
- Sick leave: 30 days full pay, 60 days 3/4 pay, 30 days unpaid per year
- Maternity leave: 10 weeks (pre/post birth)
- Iqama (residence permit + work permit): required for all expatriate employees
- Saudisation (Nitaqat): mandatory percentage of Saudi nationals in workforce; varies by sector and company size

### Personal Data Protection Law (PDPL) (Royal Decree No. M/19, 1443H / 2021)
- KSA's first comprehensive data protection law; effective September 2023
- Applies to all processing of personal data of individuals in KSA or data subjects in KSA
- Legal bases for processing: consent, contractual necessity, legal obligation, vital interests, public task, legitimate interests
- Data subject rights: access, correction, deletion, portability, objection to automated processing
- Breach notification: 72 hours to SDAIA (Saudi Data and AI Authority)
- Cross-border transfer: permitted only to adequate countries or with appropriate safeguards
- SDAIA is the regulator; NDMO (National Data Management Office) provides guidance
- Administrative fines: up to SAR 5,000,000; criminal penalties for serious violations

### Arbitration Law (Royal Decree No. M/34, 1433H / 2012)
- Based on UNCITRAL Model Law
- SCCA (Saudi Center for Commercial Arbitration): established 2016, key KSA arbitration institution
- Sharia compliance: arbitration award must not violate Sharia principles for enforcement
- Foreign awards: enforced under New York Convention (KSA acceded 1994); Sharia compliance caveat applies
- International arbitration clauses: increasingly recognised by Saudi courts post-2012 law

### Intellectual Property
- Trademark Law: registration with SAIP (Saudi Authority for Intellectual Property)
- Patent Law: 20-year protection; SAIP examination
- Copyright Law: automatic protection; term: life + 70 years for literary/artistic works
- Trade secrets: protected under Regulations for the Protection of Confidential Business Information (SAIP)
- Digital IP: increasing enforcement focus under Vision 2030

## Standard Contractual Practices
- Contracts in Arabic are mandatory for official purposes; bilingual contracts acceptable
- Arabic version prevails in Saudi courts if discrepancy between Arabic and English versions
- Riba (interest): charging compound interest or excessive interest may be challenged as contrary to Sharia
  Commercial/banking interest is permitted in regulated financial sector; conventional structures used
- Force majeure: recognised under Sharia principles and modern contract law
- Limitation periods: not codified in a single statute — varies by type of claim; practice indicates 5–10 year range
- E-signatures: Electronic Transactions Law (Royal Decree M/18, 1428H) — valid for commercial transactions
- VAT: 15% VAT rate (raised from 5% in 2020); mandatory registration for businesses exceeding SAR 375,000 revenue

## Standard NDA / Confidentiality Clause Notes
- NDA enforceable under Saudi contract law and PDPL
- Draft in Arabic or bilingual (Arabic prevails)
- Non-compete clauses: enforceable if reasonable in scope and duration; Labour Law restricts post-employment restrictions
  for lower-wage employees; commercial context allows broader restrictions
- Trade secret protection: through NDA + SAIP Trade Secret Regulations
- Islamic finance considerations: NDAs for financial transactions should avoid interest-linked provisions

## Common Risk Areas for Foreign Companies
- Commercial Agency Law: exclusive agency agreements with Saudi national agents — once registered, very difficult to terminate (as per UAE; same principle)
- Saudisation (Nitaqat): mandatory workforce quotas by sector; failure results in restrictions on visa issuances
- RHQ Programme: companies seeking Saudi government contracts from 2024 must have Regional Headquarters in KSA
- Sharia compliance: contracts, financial structures, and business activities must not violate Sharia
  (e.g., entertainment, alcohol, certain financial instruments require structuring)
- Government approvals: many sectors require MISA + sector-specific ministry approval
- Dispute resolution: local Saudi courts are the default; arbitration clause essential for commercial contracts
- Zakat: Saudi companies (and entities with Saudi shareholders) subject to Zakat (religious tax) rather than income tax
  Foreign-owned entities: subject to corporate income tax at 20%
- Withholding tax: applies to payments to non-residents (dividend, royalty, service fees); rates vary by treaty
- Anti-bribery: NAZAHA (Oversight and Anti-Corruption Authority) — strict anti-corruption enforcement
- Human rights due diligence: increasing scrutiny from international investors and counterparties

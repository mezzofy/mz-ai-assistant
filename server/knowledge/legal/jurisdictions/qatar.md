# Qatar Business Law — Reference Guide

## Legal System
Qatar operates under a civil law system influenced by Egyptian/French civil law and
Islamic Sharia principles. The constitution (2003) is the supreme law.
Sharia applies to personal status matters (family, inheritance).
Commercial law is primarily governed by codified statutes based on civil law.

**Dual system for financial services:**
1. **Onshore Qatar:** Civil law courts; Arabic language official
2. **QFC (Qatar Financial Centre):** Common law jurisdiction with its own court and regulations
   QFC Civil and Commercial Court: independent court applying English common law
   QFC Regulatory Authority (QFCRA): regulates financial services in QFC

Qatar's economy is driven by LNG (liquefied natural gas) and petrochemicals.
Post-FIFA 2022: significant diversification push in tourism, technology, logistics.

Primary courts:
- Court of First Instance
- Court of Appeal
- Court of Cassation (highest court)
- QFC Civil and Commercial Court (QFC entities)

Arbitration centres:
- QICCA (Qatar International Center for Conciliation and Arbitration): main ADR institution
- QFC Dispute Resolution Centre: for QFC-registered entities
- ICC arbitration also used for international contracts

## Key Statutes for Business

### Commercial Companies Law (Law No. 11 of 2015, as amended by Law No. 8 of 2021)
- Governs company formation and operation in Qatar
- LLC (Limited Liability Company): most common structure; minimum 2 partners, maximum 50
- Minimum capital: QAR 200,000 for LLC (verify current requirement)
- Qatarisation: 51% Qatari ownership traditionally required onshore; exceptions exist in free zones
- Investment Promotion Law No. 1 of 2019: 100% foreign ownership permitted in certain sectors/activities
- QFC: 100% foreign ownership always permitted for QFC-registered entities
- Branch offices: permitted; require commercial registration
- Holding companies: allowed; subject to specific QFC rules for holding structures

### Labour Law (Law No. 14 of 2004, as amended by Law No. 18 of 2020)
- Kafala reform (2020): significant changes — employees can change jobs without employer NOC (No Objection Certificate)
- Exit permit: abolished — employees no longer need employer permission to leave Qatar
- Minimum wage: QAR 1,000/month (with food and accommodation allowances if not provided)
- Probationary period: maximum 6 months
- Termination: 1 month notice (indefinite contracts); end of fixed-term = expiry of term
- End of service gratuity: 3 weeks' basic wage per year of service (capped at 2 years' basic wage)
- Annual leave: 3 weeks/year (<5 years); 4 weeks/year (≥5 years)
- Sick leave: 2 weeks full pay, 4 weeks half pay, 6 weeks unpaid per year
- Maternity leave: 50 days with full pay
- Work permit (RP — Residence Permit): required for all expatriate workers; employer sponsors

### Personal Data Privacy Protection Law (Law No. 13 of 2016)
- Governs collection and processing of personal data
- MOTC (Ministry of Transport and Communications) is the regulator (now Ministry of Communications and Information Technology — MCIT)
- Consent required for data collection (except in specified circumstances)
- Cross-border transfer: permitted only with adequate safeguards or MCIT approval
- QFCA Data Protection Module: applies separately to QFC-registered entities (aligned with GDPR)
- QFC Data Protection Rules 2021: GDPR-aligned requirements for QFC entities
- Penalties: criminal sanctions for unlawful data processing in onshore Qatar

### QFC-Specific Regulations
- QFC Companies Regulations: English common law governance
- QFC Employment Standards Rules: employment rights for QFC employees
- QFC Anti-Money Laundering Regulations
- QFCRA: regulates financial services, fund management, Islamic finance within QFC
- QFC entities have dual reporting: QFC + Qatar mainland requirements (where applicable)

### Arbitration Law (Law No. 2 of 2017)
- Based on UNCITRAL Model Law
- Applies to arbitrations seated in Qatar (excluding QFC)
- Foreign arbitral awards: enforceable under bilateral treaties and on reciprocity basis
- New York Convention: Qatar acceded 2003 — awards from contracting states enforceable
- QICCA: administers arbitration proceedings in Qatar

### Intellectual Property
- Trade Marks Law (Law No. 9 of 2002): registration with Ministry of Commerce and Industry
- Patents Law (Law No. 30 of 2006): 20-year protection; patent examination required
- Copyright Law (Law No. 7 of 2002): automatic protection; author's life + 50 years
- QFCA IP provisions: QFC entities follow English common law IP principles contractually

## Standard Contractual Practices
- Contracts in Arabic mandatory for official purposes; bilingual contracts acceptable
- Arabic version prevails in Qatari courts if discrepancy between versions
- QFC: English is the official language; QFC court applies English common law
- Force majeure: Civil Code provisions apply; COVID-19 and geopolitical events have tested application
- Interest: limited by Islamic finance principles in Qatari law; QFC follows commercial interest norms
- VAT: Qatar does not currently levy VAT (GCC VAT framework not yet implemented in Qatar as of 2026)
- E-signatures: Communications Technology Law permits electronic signatures for commercial transactions

## Standard NDA / Confidentiality Clause Notes
- NDA enforceable under Qatar contract law
- Draft in Arabic (onshore) or English (QFC)
- Trade secrets: protected through contractual NDA and MOCI Trade Secrets regulations
- Non-compete clauses: enforceable if reasonable; Labour Law restricts post-termination restrictions
  for employees earning below a threshold — specify scope, duration, and geography carefully
- QFC entities: common law approach — broader non-compete enforceability

## Common Risk Areas for Foreign Companies
- Qatarisation / Nationalization: some sectors require Qatari partnership (51%) — Investment Promotion Law 2019 provides exceptions for approved sectors; always verify sector-specific requirements
- QFC vs. onshore: QFC entities cannot directly trade with onshore Qatar without a separate onshore entity or agent
- Commercial Agency: exclusive agency registration — same caution as UAE/KSA; registered agents are protected
- Kafala transition: while reformed, employer sponsorship system still applies; operational compliance required
- Labour inspections: MoL actively enforces Wage Protection System (WPS) — timely salary payment mandatory
- Dispute resolution: QFC entities should include QFC dispute resolution clause; onshore contracts should specify QICCA arbitration
- Islamic finance: financial contracts for banking products must comply with AAOIFI standards or be structured as murabaha/ijara
- Anti-corruption: Law No. 11 of 2004 (Combating Corruption) — criminal liability for bribery
- Foreign currency: QAR pegged to USD at QAR 3.64 — no FX restrictions on repatriation
- Government contracts: follow procurement law (Law No. 24 of 2015) — local preference provisions apply

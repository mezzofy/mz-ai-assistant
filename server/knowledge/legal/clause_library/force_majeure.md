# Force Majeure Clauses — Standard Library

## 1. STANDARD FORCE MAJEURE
Neither Party shall be liable for any delay or failure to perform its obligations under this Agreement to the extent such failure is caused by circumstances beyond its reasonable control ("Force Majeure Event"), including but not limited to: acts of God, natural disasters, epidemics or pandemics, war, terrorism, civil unrest, government actions, regulatory changes, strikes, or failure of third-party infrastructure.

A Party affected by a Force Majeure Event shall:
(a) notify the other Party promptly (and in any event within [5] business days) of the occurrence and expected duration;
(b) use reasonable endeavours to mitigate the effects; and
(c) notify the other Party promptly when the Force Majeure Event has ended.

## 2. SUSPENSION AND TERMINATION ON FORCE MAJEURE
2.1 If a Force Majeure Event continues for more than [30 / 60] days, either Party may suspend performance of the affected obligations.
2.2 If a Force Majeure Event continues for more than [90] days, either Party may terminate this Agreement on [30] days' written notice without liability for such termination.
2.3 Payment obligations that have already accrued are not excused by a Force Majeure Event.

## 3. EXCLUSIONS
Force Majeure does not include:
(a) a Party's financial difficulty or inability to pay;
(b) change in market prices or economic conditions;
(c) a Party's failure to obtain required licences or permits (unless caused by government action);
(d) labour disputes caused by the Party's own conduct.

## Notes for Leo
- Singapore: force majeure is not a standalone statutory doctrine — must be in the contract. Courts construe FM clauses strictly. COVID-19 triggered application of COVID-19 (Temporary Measures) Act for specific contracts.
- HK: FM clauses strictly interpreted; impossibility doctrine (common law) as backup
- Malaysia: FM in contract; statutory frustration under Contracts Act 1950 for performance impossible
- UAE (Civil Code): Article 273 provides statutory frustration doctrine (impossibility). Civil Code FM provision supplements contractual FM clause.
- UAE (DIFC): common law frustration doctrine applies; contractual FM clause takes precedence
- Saudi Arabia: force majeure / FM recognised under Saudi contract law; Sharia principle of darurah (necessity) applies
- Qatar: Civil Code Article 188 — statutory impossibility doctrine. Contractual FM clause typically drafted to supplement.
